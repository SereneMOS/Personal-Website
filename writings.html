Morning Dove
One day, you’re going to see her again,
Maybe at the movies, maybe at the park-
Maybe when you’re long dead and rotting
But on that day, she will be as wide-eyed as ever
And I don’t think she’ll recognize you
But you’ll both know its each other
I wonder what you’ll say to her
I wouldn’t be too nervous
Because she’s going to adore you
But despite her admiration
You’ll be besieged with nothing but grief,
Because all you’ll be able to see
Is all of her defilement
And you’ll want to say sorry
For everything that happened,
Even though it wasn’t your fault
And you’ll want to say sorry
For trying so hard to bury her- for calling her naive,
When you didn’t know who to blame
And you’ll want to say sorry
That it wasn’t you who looked after her,
Or that you couldn’t live out her dreams
I wonder how she’ll react
So on that day, when you see her
Give her a hug for me, please?

Charcot-Wilbrand
  It’s not the kind of thing anyone would have noticed right away. I mean, how could it have been? Slowly but surely, however, people took notice. Maybe it was a lucky guess, an interruption in a pattern too rigid to go unnoticed when disturbed, or, given the circumstances, likely divine prognostication. Those first few to say something were passed off as all sorts of things-– ”up the pole”, mistaken, or, in the best of cases, assumed to be joking–-but their words planted a seed, a viral notion that would inevitably take hold. In a few days time, after mocking the inane rumor over lunch with coworkers or on the bus with friends, something wouldn’t sit right. Next time, you’d be sure to remember if it happened, and dread would start to set in when you woke. Everyone you met would start parroting the same thing, the same troubling idea.
  As the world at large came to see these ramblings as truth, there was panic. For some, the change didn’t affect them at all, but obviously, the thought was spine chilling nonetheless. Religious figures, scientists, politicians, philosophers, and other swaths of humanity drew their own conclusions, and tried to settle the nerves.
  “‘Tis the judgment of God upon the wicked,” claimed one pastor.
  “A new virus, perhaps?” postulated a scientist.
  “Must be a weapon from the Chinese,” asserted military leadership.
  Try as they might, and for as confident as they all sounded, none knew the truth. However, the seasons changed, as did the interest of most people. Since nothing bad had come from it, it must be fine, right?
  And so, years passed, then decades, and all of the world started to forget what had even happened. It would be of great intrigue to the historians of the day to piece together such a significant event. How could humankind have been so foolish as to not understand the gravity of the situation? I suppose hindsight is 20/20. And so, through vigorous work, the appraisal of the remnants of their predecessors, and a little bit of educated guessing, these antiquarians pinpointed it: the day humanity lost something it would never again recover. The day that slumber would then and forever be met by a void.
  The day humanity stopped dreaming.

Witchcraft and Naginata
Wow, it has been a hot minute, hasn't it? The past few months have been keeping me busy, what with the fall semester in full swing and whatnot, but with a whirlwind of things going on that means there's no shortage of things to blog about!
Two things have been getting me through the thick of it have been my burgeoning practice of Witchcraft and Naginatajutsu. I don't see myself performing or competing or anything, but its a fun skill to learn, and a good way to get some needed exercise in.
The next big shift has come from my newfound practice of witchcraft! Don't fret-- I won't be sacrificing infants or flying around on brooms (probably), but rather exploring esoterica and the lingering power of the subconscious. There's no need to go into everything about the practice or history, especially since practice is so individualized, but just know that the change in my perspective has helped me recitfy a lot of the stray thoughts or conundrums that have been bouncing around my head for a long time now.

Homestuck: The First Half
It's hard to really know what to say about Homestuck when introudcing it. By this point in internet history you've really either heard about it and know the strange baggage the series has, or you've never heard of it at all-- at least for those of us born around the turn of the century, I'd argue. Even then, most people haven't done the legwork of sitting down and reading all 8000+ pages of the webcoming, and fewer still have done so twice as I have. I should also clear the air and let it be known that these readings were both done within the past couple of years, and prior I had known only the things picked up through cultural osmosis like everyone else.
That said, a lot of what I read stuck with me in a very profound way, to the point where I may have to confess that I actually like Homestuck? It's hard to say. I decided to break this review into two parts, covering everything up to EOA4 here, since there's just an insane amount of material to cover and the second half of HS is pretty different from the first.
Homestuck starts slow. It's clear the author, Andrew Hussie, wasn't sure what to do at first, so the story meanders a little bit in the first few moves, and it does seem like this is where most people fall off during their read. That said, a lot of good characterization happens here, with introductions to our four main characters: John, Rose, Dave, and Jade, it's just that more of this characterization is actually retroactive than you'd probably expect. It's not that nothing is happening- I mean, the whole first part of the story is about Earth being destroyed by meteors and the protagonists have to use a video game to escape the same fate- it's just that there's a lot of jumping around diversions that slow things down. This is what Homestuck is, and its entirely understandable why that wouldn't be a lot of people's thing; everything I am about to discuss in this post happens in one day, canonically.
